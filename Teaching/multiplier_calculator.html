üí° Economic Multiplier Theory

The multiplier effect describes how initial changes in spending lead to larger changes in total economic output. When the government increases spending by $1, the total increase in GDP is typically greater than $1 due to subsequent rounds of spending.

üèõÔ∏è Government Spending Multiplier
ŒîGDP = ŒîG √ó K

Where:

  • ŒîGDP = Change in Gross Domestic Product
  • ŒîG = Change in Government Spending
  • K = Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC) or given directly
üìã Tax Multiplier
ŒîGDP = ŒîT √ó c √ó K

Where:

  • ŒîT = Change in Taxes (negative for tax cuts)
  • c = Marginal Propensity to Consume from tax changes
  • K = Multiplier

Note: Tax cuts have a smaller multiplier effect than direct government spending because people save some portion of their tax savings.

üìä Budget Balance
Budget Balance = T - G

Where:

  • T = Tax Revenue = t √ó GDP
  • G = Government Spending
  • t = Tax Rate (as decimal)

Surplus: T > G (positive balance)
Deficit: T < G (negative balance)
Balanced: T = G (zero balance)

üìà Aggregate Demand Effects

Tax Cuts: Increase disposable income ‚Üí Higher consumption ‚Üí AD shifts right ‚Üí Higher GDP and price level

Tax Increases: Reduce disposable income ‚Üí Lower consumption ‚Üí AD shifts left ‚Üí Lower GDP and price level

Government Spending: Direct increase in aggregate demand ‚Üí AD shifts right ‚Üí Multiplier effect amplifies impact
